Holding a Variable Constant

restrictions - none, you can do this at anytime

When you hold a variable constant, you will force any derivative of that constant in the equation to be reduced to 0, for example, let P be some function of V and T,

P=f(V,T) P = f(V,T) P=f(V,T)

note that the partial derivatives of P (indicated with a δ\deltaδ symbol) are not effected by holding the variable constant.

Non-Natural Derivatives

when - you can do this at anytime
restrictions - none

What if we know that U is some function of S and V,

U=f(S,V) U = f(S,V) U=f(S,V)

but we don't know exactly how f(S,V)f(S,V)f(S,V) is defined (for the sake of this example). We want to find out how the internal energy of the system changes with volume while being held at constant pressure,

(δUδV)P= ?\left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_P =~ ? (δVδU​)P​= ?

the total derivative of U,

dU=(δUδS)VdS+(δUδV)SdV dU = \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta S}\right)_VdS+\left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_SdVdU=(δSδU​)V​dS+(δVδU​)S​dV

At first glance, this seems to be an impossible problem to solve for, since internal energy, U, does not depend on pressure. How can you solve for something that isn't even defined in the original function? This process is refereed to as a non-natural derivative (sometimes called unnatural).

First, divide both sides by dV

dUdV=(δUδS)VdSdV+(δUδV)S \frac{dU}{dV} = \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta S}\right)_V\frac{dS}{dV}+\left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_SdVdU​=(δSδU​)V​dVdS​+(δVδU​)S​

then hold P constant, any partial derivative that is already being held constant is not effected by this,

(δUδV)P=(δUδS)V(δSδV)P+(δUδV)S \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_P = \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta S}\right)_V\left(\frac{\delta S}{\delta V}\right)_P+\left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_S(δVδU​)P​=(δSδU​)V​(δVδS​)P​+(δVδU​)S​

At this point we have achieved our objective, we have an expression for the change internal energy with respect to volume at constant pressure without knowing the exact nature of f(S,V)f(S,V)f(S,V).

Why Non-Natural Derivatives Are Important

Sometimes the expressions that we derive are not easy to measure (if possible at all) but by careful inspection we discover some unlikely things. Consider internal energy again that U=f(S,V)U = f(S,V)U=f(S,V). We actually do know how f(S,V)f(S,V)f(S,V) is defined (because we can define it however we want).

U=f(S,V)=TS−PV U = f(S,V) = TS - PV U=f(S,V)=TS−PV
dU=TdS−PdV dU = TdS - PdVdU=TdS−PdV
dUdV=TdSdV−P \frac{dU}{dV} = T\frac{dS}{dV} - PdVdU​=TdVdS​−P

Consider the derivative of internal energy and then hold P constant

(δUδV)P=T(δSδV)P−P \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_P = T\left(\frac{\delta S}{\delta V}\right)_P- P(δVδU​)P​=T(δVδS​)P​−P

looking back at our non-natural derivative results of U=f(S,V)U = f(S,V)U=f(S,V),

(δUδV)P=(δUδS)V(δSδV)P+(δUδV)S \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_P = \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta S}\right)_V\left(\frac{\delta S}{\delta V}\right)_P+\left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_S(δVδU​)P​=(δSδU​)V​(δVδS​)P​+(δVδU​)S​

it becomes clear (not really but it makes us sound smart) that,

T=(δUδS)V T = \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta S}\right)_V T=(δSδU​)V​

and

P=−(δUδV)S P = -\left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_S P=−(δVδU​)S​

substituting them back into the non-natural derivative result,

(δUδV)P=T(δSδV)P+P \left(\frac{\delta U}{\delta V}\right)_P = T\left(\frac{\delta S}{\delta V}\right)_P+P(δVδU​)P​=T(δVδS​)P​+P

let P become a variable again,

dUdV=TdSdV+P \frac{dU}{dV} = T\frac{dS}{dV}+PdVdU​=TdVdS​+P

multiply both sides by dV,

dU=TdS−PdV dU = TdS - PdVdU=TdS−PdV

and we have come full circle but we now have some fundamental expressions for two very macroscopic quantities, which is very cool and neat.
